BMEN90033 · WEEK 9 · SAMPLE-AND-HOLD

Freezing the input before conversion.

A successive-approximation ADC takes one bit of decision per clock cycle. If the input drifts during conversion, the comparator decisions made early in the sequence no longer correspond to the value reaching the comparator at the end. The sample-and-hold circuit prevents this by capturing the input on a small capacitor and presenting that captured value to the converter for the full duration of the conversion.

01why hold the sample

A SAR ADC needs a stationary input.

The successive-approximation algorithm refines a digital estimate of $V_\mathrm{in}$ one bit at a time. Each comparison sets one bit of the output, and that bit remains fixed in all subsequent cycles. The algorithm is therefore valid only if the same $V_\mathrm{in}$ is presented to the comparator on every cycle.

The panel on the right runs an $N=8$ bit SAR conversion on a slowly drifting input. With the sample-and-hold disabled, the DAC trajectory chases a moving target and the final code differs from the value the input had at the start of the conversion. With the sample-and-hold enabled, the input is captured at the start of the conversion and the algorithm converges as intended.

Toggle the S/H switch and observe how the final code aligns with the captured value rather than the instantaneous input.

The maximum input slew that a SAR can tolerate without a front-end S/H is roughly $\mathrm{d}V/\mathrm{d}t < \tfrac{1}{2}\mathrm{LSB} / T_\mathrm{conv}$. For a 12-bit converter running at $\,1\,\mathrm{MS\,s^{-1}}$ on a $1\,\mathrm{V}$ range, this is about $0.12\,\mathrm{V\,ms^{-1}}$, which any signal of clinical interest exceeds easily.

// track and hold
02two operating phases

Tracking the input, then holding the last value.

A sample-and-hold alternates between two phases under the control of a clock $\phi$. During the track phase, a switch closes and a small hold capacitor $C_H$ follows the input. During the hold phase, the switch opens and the capacitor retains the voltage it had at the moment of opening, since there is nowhere for charge to flow.

The capacitor voltage on the held output is therefore a piecewise function: a continuous segment that follows $V_\mathrm{in}$ during track, and a flat segment at the captured value during hold. The downstream ADC performs its conversion entirely within the hold phase.

Use the sliders to vary the sampling rate and the fraction of the period spent tracking. As the sample rate increases, the staircase of held values approaches the input more closely.

If the track phase is shorter than the time required for $C_H$ to settle, the captured value is biased toward the previous sample. The minimum track time is set by the acquisition time derived in the next section.

03circuit topology

An input buffer, a switch, a capacitor, and an output buffer.

The canonical sample-and-hold places a series MOSFET switch between two unity-gain buffers. The input buffer drives the switch with low source impedance so the hold capacitor can charge quickly. The output buffer presents a high impedance to the capacitor so that during hold the only discharge path is through the buffer's input bias current and the switch's leakage.

The hold capacitor $C_H$ lives at the buffered switch output. Its value is a compromise: a larger $C_H$ reduces droop and is less affected by charge injection, but takes longer to acquire the input.

Click the gate of the MOSFET to toggle the clock manually. Charging current flows into $C_H$ only while the switch is closed; in the hold phase the capacitor is isolated and $V_C$ is frozen.

In practice, the switch is implemented as a transmission gate (an N-channel and a P-channel MOSFET in parallel) so that the on-resistance remains roughly constant across the full input range. The principle is unchanged.

04acquisition time

Charging $C_H$ through the on-resistance.

When the switch closes onto an input that has changed since the previous track phase, the hold capacitor charges through the switch's on-resistance. The capacitor voltage follows $$ V_C(t) = V_\mathrm{in} \left( 1 - e^{-t/\tau} \right), \quad \tau = R_\mathrm{on}\,C_H. $$

The acquisition time $t_\mathrm{acq}$ is the time required for $V_C$ to settle within an acceptable fraction of the final value. For an $N$-bit ADC, settling to within $\tfrac{1}{2}\mathrm{LSB}$ requires $t_\mathrm{acq} \approx \tau (N + 1) \ln 2$. Track phases shorter than this leave the capacitor at a value biased toward the previous sample.

Adjust $R_\mathrm{on}$ and $C_H$ and observe how the time constant $\tau$ scales the charging curve. The maximum sample rate consistent with full settling within a $30\%$ track duty is reported on the right.

The trade-off is clear: a small $C_H$ acquires quickly but droops faster in hold; a large $C_H$ holds well but limits the sample rate. Typical hold capacitors in monolithic S/H amplifiers are $1$ to $10\,\mathrm{pF}$.

05non-idealities

Droop, aperture jitter, and charge injection.

A real sample-and-hold departs from the ideal in three ways. None is fatal in isolation; together they set the practical resolution and sample rate of the front end.

Droop

During hold, the capacitor discharges slowly through the leakage current of the switch and the input bias of the output buffer. The held voltage falls at a rate $\mathrm{d}V/\mathrm{d}t = I_\mathrm{leak} / C_H$. Across a conversion window of $T_\mathrm{conv}$, this produces a droop of $\Delta V = I_\mathrm{leak}\,T_\mathrm{conv} / C_H$.

Aperture jitter

The clock edge that opens the switch has its own timing noise. If the input is changing at $\mathrm{d}V/\mathrm{d}t$ at the moment of opening, a jitter $\sigma_t$ on the clock translates to a voltage error $\sigma_V = \sigma_t \cdot \mathrm{d}V/\mathrm{d}t$. Aperture jitter sets the ultimate signal-to-noise ratio of high-frequency converters.

Charge injection

When the MOSFET turns off, channel charge is dumped onto $C_H$. This produces a small step $\Delta V_\mathrm{cj} \approx Q_\mathrm{ch} / (2 C_H)$ on every hold transition. A dummy switch of half the size, driven by the inverted clock, cancels most of the injected charge.

06feeding the converter

The held value is what the SAR sees.

The output of the sample-and-hold is the input to the converter. From the SAR's point of view, $V_\mathrm{in}$ is constant for the entire conversion window, regardless of how the true input is moving. The staircase shown in the panel is the sequence of held values, each delivered to the converter for one full sample period.

This is the assumption the next page makes implicitly. The sampling, quantisation, and SAR algorithm scenes in Analog-to-Digital Conversion all operate on the held output, not on the raw input.

The combination of a sample-and-hold and a SAR ADC is so universal that monolithic SAR converters integrate the S/H on the same die, often as a charge-redistribution capacitor array that doubles as both the sampling capacitor and the conversion DAC.
