BMEN90033 · WEEK 7 · BIOINSTRUMENTATION

Removing powerline noise.

The canonical decoupling network for an operational amplifier consists of a bulk capacitor, commonly $10\,\mu\mathrm{F}$, placed in parallel with a small ceramic, commonly $0.1\,\mu\mathrm{F}$, at each supply terminal. Under the ideal capacitor model, the aggregate capacitance is $10.1\,\mu\mathrm{F}$ and the small device contributes negligibly. Yet omission of the small capacitor is observed to degrade the power-supply rejection of precision analog front-ends and, in extreme cases, to precipitate instability. The apparent paradox is resolved by recognising that physical capacitors exhibit parasitic inductance and resistance, and that the two components in the standard pair occupy disjoint regions of the frequency axis.

01why op-amps need clean rails

The supply terminal as an input.

The output voltage of an operational amplifier is synthesised from charge drawn from its positive and negative supply rails. Because the transistors of the output stage exhibit finite transconductance and finite output conductance, any perturbation of the rail potential is transferred to the output with a non-zero, frequency-dependent coupling coefficient.

This coupling is quantified by the power-supply rejection ratio (PSRR), defined as the ratio of a perturbation at the supply to the resulting perturbation at the output, referred to the input. Precision devices typically exhibit a low-frequency PSRR on the order of $100\,\mathrm{dB}$. Above a break frequency of a few hundred hertz, however, the rejection falls at approximately $20\,\mathrm{dB}$ per decade, reaching $20\,\mathrm{dB}$ in the vicinity of $1\,\mathrm{MHz}$ and vanishing, for practical purposes, above $10\,\mathrm{MHz}$. In the high-frequency band the amplifier therefore provides little attenuation between supply perturbations and the output signal.

The sources of such perturbations are intrinsic to the system. Switch-mode regulators inject a fundamental at the switching frequency, commonly $500\,\mathrm{kHz}$ to several megahertz, together with its harmonics. Digital logic elsewhere on the printed circuit board draws pulsed currents through the distributed inductance of the supply network, producing wideband transient voltages. The output stage of the amplifier itself demands transient current which the upstream regulator, whose control loop typically has a bandwidth of a few kilohertz, cannot supply within the relevant time scale.

Bypass capacitor. A bypass capacitor provides a low-impedance path from the supply terminal to the reference potential. It limits rail-voltage disturbances and supplies transient load current locally.

02the textbook capacitor

Under the ideal model, a single capacitor would suffice.

The impedance of the ideal capacitor is

$$Z_C(j\omega) = \frac{1}{j\omega C}, \qquad |Z_C| = \frac{1}{2\pi f C}.$$

On logarithmic axes the magnitude response is a straight line of slope $-20\,\mathrm{dB}$ per decade, so that each decade of frequency yields a corresponding decade of impedance reduction. Under this model a $10\,\mu\mathrm{F}$ capacitor presents $1.6\,\Omega$ at $10\,\mathrm{kHz}$, $160\,\mathrm{m}\Omega$ at $100\,\mathrm{kHz}$, and $16\,\mathrm{m}\Omega$ at $1\,\mathrm{MHz}$. It would follow that a single large capacitor provides the lowest attainable bypass impedance at every frequency, and that augmenting it with a $0.1\,\mu\mathrm{F}$ capacitor in parallel, whose ideal impedance exceeds that of the bulk device by two orders of magnitude at all frequencies, can provide only a marginal improvement.

Were the ideal model exact, the aggregate capacitance would indeed be $10.1\,\mu\mathrm{F}$, the combination would present the impedance of a slightly enlarged single capacitor, and the smaller device would serve no identifiable purpose. Measured impedance spectra of physical capacitors demonstrate otherwise.

$$Z_{\text{parallel}}(j\omega) = \frac{1}{j\omega(C_1 + C_2)} \quad {\scriptsize\text{(ideal model; valid only in the low-frequency limit).}}$$

03the real component

The physical capacitor is also an inductor.

A physical capacitor departs from the ideal model in two systematic respects. Its terminals, internal electrodes, and interconnect contribute a non-zero loop area through which the charging and discharging currents must flow, and this loop possesses a finite self-inductance. Dissipative mechanisms in the dielectric, the electrodes, and the terminations contribute a non-zero series resistance. The component is accordingly represented by the lumped series connection of the nominal capacitance $C$, an equivalent series inductance $\mathrm{ESL}$, and an equivalent series resistance $\mathrm{ESR}$:

equivalent circuit of a real capacitor

$R_p$ is the dielectric leakage resistance, typically $\mathrm{M\Omega}$ to $\mathrm{T\Omega}$. At bypass frequencies its admittance is negligible compared to $j\omega C$, so it is omitted from the $Z(j\omega)$ expression below.

$$Z(j\omega) = \mathrm{ESR} + j\omega\,\mathrm{ESL} + \frac{1}{j\omega C}.$$

The corresponding magnitude response exhibits a V-shaped profile on logarithmic axes. At sufficiently low frequencies the term $1/(\omega C)$ is dominant and the impedance decreases at $-20\,\mathrm{dB}$ per decade, in accordance with the ideal model. At sufficiently high frequencies the term $\omega\,\mathrm{ESL}$ is dominant, the impedance increases at $+20\,\mathrm{dB}$ per decade, and the component behaves as a series inductor. The transition between these two regimes occurs at the self-resonant frequency,

$$f_{\text{SRF}} = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{\mathrm{ESL}\cdot C}},$$

at which the inductive and capacitive reactances cancel exactly, and the impedance attains a minimum equal to the $\mathrm{ESR}$.

Scaling of self-resonance with capacitance

The quantity of central interest is the dependence of $f_{\text{SRF}}$ on $C$ when the device geometry is varied accordingly. An electrolytic or tantalum capacitor of $10\,\mu\mathrm{F}$ typically occupies a comparatively large case, possesses an extended internal current path, and exhibits an $\mathrm{ESL}$ of order $5$ to $10\,\mathrm{nH}$. The corresponding self-resonant frequency is

$$f_{\text{SRF,bulk}} \approx \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{(5\,\mathrm{nH})(10\,\mu\mathrm{F})}} \approx 710\,\mathrm{kHz}.$$

Above this frequency the device no longer behaves capacitively, and its impedance increases with frequency. A ceramic capacitor of $0.1\,\mu\mathrm{F}$ in an 0603 or 0402 surface-mount package presents a short internal current path and an $\mathrm{ESL}$ of approximately $0.5$ to $1\,\mathrm{nH}$. Its self-resonant frequency is therefore

$$f_{\text{SRF,cer}} \approx \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{(1\,\mathrm{nH})(0.1\,\mu\mathrm{F})}} \approx 16\,\mathrm{MHz}.$$

The ceramic, notwithstanding its two-order-of-magnitude smaller capacitance, is self-resonant at a frequency more than twenty times higher than the electrolytic. This follows from the dependence of $f_{\text{SRF}}$ on the geometric mean of $\mathrm{ESL}$ and $C$: the reduction in capacitance by a factor of one hundred is partially offset by a concurrent reduction in parasitic inductance by a factor of approximately five.

The two components have distinct frequency ranges. The $10\,\mu\mathrm{F}$ device provides the lower impedance at low and intermediate frequencies. Above its self-resonance it becomes inductive, while the $0.1\,\mu\mathrm{F}$ ceramic remains capacitive to a higher frequency.

04the parallel combination

The pair $10\,\mu\mathrm{F} \parallel 0.1\,\mu\mathrm{F}$ does not present $10.1\,\mu\mathrm{F}$.

The parallel connection of the two devices yields an admittance equal to the sum of the individual admittances, from which the composite impedance follows:

$$Y_{\text{tot}}(j\omega) = Y_{\text{bulk}}(j\omega) + Y_{\text{cer}}(j\omega), \qquad Z_{\text{tot}}(j\omega) = \frac{1}{Y_{\text{tot}}(j\omega)}.$$

At any given frequency, the current distributes between the two branches in inverse proportion to their impedances, so that the composite response is governed by the branch of lower impedance. Below the self-resonance of the bulk device, the $10\,\mu\mathrm{F}$ dominates, since its capacitive reactance is substantially smaller than that of the $0.1\,\mu\mathrm{F}$. Above the bulk self-resonance, the impedance of the $10\,\mu\mathrm{F}$ device increases with frequency while that of the $0.1\,\mu\mathrm{F}$ continues to decrease toward its own self-resonance; at the frequency at which the two impedances are equal, the ceramic assumes the role of principal conductor for high-frequency noise currents. The composite impedance therefore follows the lower envelope of the two individual curves at every frequency, producing an extended interval of low impedance that neither component is capable of providing in isolation.

The composite does not behave as a $10.1\,\mu\mathrm{F}$ capacitor. The effective bypass capacitance at any frequency is determined by whichever component presents the lower impedance at that frequency, not by the arithmetic sum of the nominal values. The additive rule is valid exclusively in the low-frequency limit, in which both components are well described by the ideal model and the contribution of the smaller capacitor is demonstrably negligible. Above a few hundred kilohertz the bulk component contributes no useful bypass admittance, and the composite behaves as a $0.1\,\mu\mathrm{F}$ capacitor with $\mathrm{ESL} \approx 1\,\mathrm{nH}$ rather than as a $10.1\,\mu\mathrm{F}$ capacitor with any meaningful effective inductance.

Consequences for amplifier operation. The output stage draws transient current above the self-resonant frequency of the bulk capacitor. A nearby ceramic capacitor supplies this high-frequency current and reduces the resulting rail-voltage disturbance. The bulk capacitor restores charge at lower frequencies, where its larger capacitance provides the lower impedance.

Implications for printed circuit board layout

  • The ceramic capacitor must be placed in immediate proximity to the supply terminal of the amplifier. Any interconnecting trace introduces additional loop inductance in series with the intrinsic $\mathrm{ESL}$ of the component, which depresses the self-resonant frequency and raises the minimum attainable bypass impedance across its useful bandwidth.
  • The positional constraint on the bulk capacitor is less stringent, as it governs mid-frequency decoupling rather than the critical high-frequency regime. Trace lengths of several centimetres are typically tolerable.
  • The parallel connection of multiple ceramic capacitors of a given value reduces the aggregate $\mathrm{ESL}$ in inverse proportion to the number of devices. The mechanism is identical to that of the bulk–ceramic pair, applied within a single decade of capacitance.
  • The effective capacitance of class-2 ceramic dielectrics, including X7R and Y5V, is a strong function of the applied DC bias and of temperature. Devices intended to provide a specified high-frequency impedance should be selected with a voltage rating of at least twice the nominal supply potential, and the derated capacitance should be verified against the manufacturer's bias characteristic.

Capacitance value alone does not determine bypass performance. Package geometry and construction determine the current-path inductance and therefore the useful frequency range. A sufficiently large ceramic capacitor with low ESL can replace a separate bulk and ceramic pair when its bias-dependent capacitance remains adequate.

05transfer function representation

Confirmation by the composite transfer function.

The qualitative argument of the preceding section admits a formal confirmation through the transfer function representation of the bypass network. Treated as a linear time-invariant two-terminal element, each capacitor branch is characterised by an impedance expressible as a rational function of the Laplace variable $s$.

Branch impedance

The series connection of $\mathrm{ESR}_k$, $\mathrm{ESL}_k$, and $C_k$ yields, for the $k$-th branch,

$$\begin{aligned} Z_k(s) &= \mathrm{ESR}_k + s\,\mathrm{ESL}_k + \frac{1}{sC_k} = \frac{D_k(s)}{sC_k}, \\[14pt] \text{where}\quad D_k(s) &\equiv s^2\,\mathrm{ESL}_k C_k + s\,\mathrm{ESR}_k C_k + 1. \end{aligned}$$

The branch admittance $Y_k(s) = sC_k / D_k(s)$ therefore possesses a simple zero at the origin and a pair of poles at the roots of $D_k(s)$. Under the low-damping condition $\mathrm{ESR}_k^2 < 4\,\mathrm{ESL}_k/C_k$, these roots form a complex-conjugate pair situated in the left half-plane with natural frequency $\omega_{0,k} = 1/\sqrt{\mathrm{ESL}_k C_k}$, precisely the self-resonant frequency of the branch.

Composite impedance

Since admittances are additive in parallel, the composite admittance is

$$Y_{\text{tot}}(s) = Y_1(s) + Y_2(s) = \frac{s\,\bigl[C_1 D_2(s) + C_2 D_1(s)\bigr]}{D_1(s)\,D_2(s)},$$

and the composite impedance is therefore the rational function

$$Z_{\text{tot}}(s) = \frac{D_1(s)\,D_2(s)}{s\,\bigl[C_1 D_2(s) + C_2 D_1(s)\bigr]}.$$

The numerator is of degree four and the denominator of degree three; $Z_{\text{tot}}(s)$ is accordingly a proper rational function possessing four zeros and three poles, whose configuration in the $s$-plane admits the following interpretation.

Zero structure

The zeros of $Z_{\text{tot}}(s)$ are the roots of $D_1(s)$ and $D_2(s)$, coinciding with the self-resonant frequencies of the two individual branches. Each conjugate pair lies close to the imaginary axis at a distance $\alpha_k = \mathrm{ESR}_k / (2\,\mathrm{ESL}_k)$ determined by the dissipation of the corresponding branch. On the imaginary axis, these zeros manifest as the two local minima of $|Z_{\text{tot}}(j\omega)|$ observed in the plot of the preceding section.

Pole structure and anti-resonance

The denominator factorises into a trivial pole at $s = 0$, corresponding to the open-circuit behaviour of capacitors at DC, and the roots of the quadratic

$$C_1 C_2 (\mathrm{ESL}_1 + \mathrm{ESL}_2)\,s^2 + C_1 C_2 (\mathrm{ESR}_1 + \mathrm{ESR}_2)\,s + (C_1 + C_2) = 0.$$

In the underdamped regime these roots constitute a complex-conjugate pair with natural frequency

$$\small \omega_{\text{AR}} = \sqrt{\frac{C_1 + C_2}{C_1 C_2\,(\mathrm{ESL}_1 + \mathrm{ESL}_2)}} \;\approx\; \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mathrm{ESL}_1\,C_2}} \quad \bigl(C_1 \gg C_2,\; \mathrm{ESL}_1 \gg \mathrm{ESL}_2\bigr).$$

The approximation is instructive: the resonance of the composite network is governed by the equivalent series inductance of the bulk device and the capacitance of the ceramic device. Physically, this is the frequency at which the post-resonance inductive reactance of the bulk capacitor and the pre-resonance capacitive reactance of the ceramic capacitor combine to form a parallel LC tank. For the nominal parameters of the present analysis this yields $f_{\text{AR}} \approx 6.5\,\mathrm{MHz}$, situated between the two self-resonances.

A conjugate pole pair of small real part produces a local maximum of $|Z_{\text{tot}}(j\omega)|$ at $\omega \approx \omega_{\text{AR}}$. This anti-resonance lies between the two self-resonant minima. The combined ESR damps the parallel tank.

Agreement with the measured behaviour. The transfer function analysis reproduces the two qualitative features identified in Section 04: the composite impedance traces the lower envelope of the two individual curves away from the anti-resonance (a consequence of the zeros of $Z_{\text{tot}}$ being inherited from the individual branches), and the effective capacitance of the pair at any frequency is dictated by the branch presenting the lower impedance, not by the arithmetic sum $C_1 + C_2$. The additive rule $C_{\text{eff}} = C_1 + C_2$ is recovered only in the limit $s \to 0$, where both $D_k(s) \to 1$ and $Z_{\text{tot}}(s) \to 1/[s(C_1 + C_2)]$.
