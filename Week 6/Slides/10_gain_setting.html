If the closed-loop gain is simply set by the ratio R2/R1, does it matter what actual values I choose for R2 and R1?
Correct: B

The gain equation ACL = R2/R1 only cares about the ratio, but practical constraints set a useful range — typically 10 kΩ to 100 kΩ:

Too low (< 1 kΩ): The op-amp output must drive heavy current through low-value resistors. Most op-amps can only source ~20–25 mA, so small resistances cause the output to clip, distort, or overheat.

Too high (> 1 MΩ): Input bias currents (nA–μA) flowing through large resistances create significant DC offset voltages at the output. Additionally, stray board capacitance (~pF) in parallel with high-value resistors forms unintended low-pass filters that limit bandwidth. Johnson (thermal) noise also scales with √R.

The "sweet spot" of 10 kΩ–100 kΩ keeps current draw manageable, offsets small, and parasitic effects negligible.
