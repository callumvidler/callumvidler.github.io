The Op-Amp as a Transfer Function.

The operational amplifier is a two-input, one-output device whose open-loop dynamics are dominated by a single pole. Applying negative feedback relocates that pole, trading gain for bandwidth while preserving the gain–bandwidth product. This page derives both transfer functions and visualises the resulting $s$-plane geometry and time response.

01the device

The open-loop op-amp.

In its open-loop configuration the op-amp produces an output proportional to the differential input, $v_d = v_+ - v_-$. For an ideal device this gain is infinite; for a real device it is finite, frequency-dependent, and limited by internal compensation.

The circuit to the right depicts the open-loop connection: a non-inverting input $v_+$ driven by a source $v_{in}(t)$, an inverting input $v_-$ returned to ground, and an output $v_o$ referenced to the same ground.

Ideal assumptions

  • Infinite differential gain, $A_0 \to \infty$ at DC.
  • Infinite input impedance, $i_+ = i_- = 0$.
  • Zero output impedance, output voltage independent of load.
  • Infinite bandwidth and zero offset.

Real devices depart from these limits. The finite DC gain $A_0$ lies typically between $10^5$ and $10^6$, and the response rolls off above a low dominant-pole frequency $\omega_a$, introduced deliberately during fabrication to guarantee stability under feedback.

Dominant-pole compensation. A small integrated capacitor between internal gain stages produces a single low-frequency pole. All higher-order poles lie well above the unity-gain frequency, so the device behaves as first-order across its useful range.

02one-pole model

From first principles.

The dominant-pole compensation capacitor $C_c$ converts the internal gain stage into a single-pole low-pass. The output responds to the differential input through a first-order linear ordinary differential equation, from which the transfer function follows directly.

showderivation · 4 steps▶

Step 1 · governing equation

Let $g_m$ denote the transconductance of the input stage and $R_o$ the output resistance across which the compensation capacitor $C_c$ appears in parallel. A differential input $v_d$ drives a current $g_m v_d$ into the parallel combination:

$$C_c\,\dot{v}_o + \frac{v_o}{R_o} = g_m\,v_d.$$

Step 2 · Laplace transform

Under quiescent initial conditions, the Laplace transform yields:

$$\bigl(sC_c + 1/R_o\bigr)\,V_o(s) = g_m\,V_d(s).$$

Step 3 · solve for the ratio

Rearranging and dividing numerator and denominator by $1/R_o$:

$$A(s) = \frac{V_o(s)}{V_d(s)} = \frac{g_m R_o}{1 + sR_oC_c}.$$

Step 4 · canonical parameters

Identifying $A_0 = g_m R_o$ (DC open-loop gain) and $\omega_a = 1/(R_oC_c)$ (dominant-pole frequency):

$$A(s) = \frac{A_0}{1 + s/\omega_a}$$

The transfer function is strictly first-order, with a single pole at $s = -\omega_a$ and no finite zeros. The product of DC gain and pole frequency,

$$\omega_t \;=\; A_0\,\omega_a,$$

is the unity-gain bandwidth (or gain–bandwidth product, GBW). It is an intrinsic device parameter, invariant under feedback, and typically quoted directly in data sheets (e.g. $\omega_t/2\pi \approx 1\text{ MHz}$ for a general-purpose part).

Block diagram

In the open-loop case the block diagram contains a single forward element $A(s)$; the input is the differential voltage $V_d(s)$ and the output is $V_o(s)$.

03open-loop behaviour

One pole. Slow decay.

The open-loop transfer function possesses a single real pole at $s = -\omega_a$ on the negative real axis. Because $\omega_a$ is small (typically $\omega_a/2\pi \sim 10\text{ Hz}$), this pole lies very close to the imaginary axis; the associated decay time constant $\tau = 1/\omega_a$ is long.

$$p_{\text{ol}} = -\omega_a, \qquad \tau_{\text{ol}} = \frac{1}{\omega_a}$$

The impulse response follows directly from the inverse Laplace transform:

$$h(t) = A_0\,\omega_a\,e^{-\omega_a t}\,u(t).$$

A step input of magnitude $V_s$ produces a saturating exponential approach to $A_0 V_s$:

$$v_o(t) = A_0 V_s\bigl(1 - e^{-\omega_a t}\bigr).$$

In practice the output saturates at the supply rails long before reaching $A_0 V_s$; open-loop operation is therefore useful only as a comparator. Meaningful linear amplification requires feedback.

04negative feedback

Returning a fraction of the output.

Applying negative feedback returns a scaled copy of the output to the inverting input. The canonical non-inverting amplifier accomplishes this through a resistive divider $\{R_1, R_2\}$ that sets the feedback factor

$$\beta = \frac{R_1}{R_1 + R_2}.$$

The input signal is applied to $v_+$ directly, and the inverting terminal samples the fraction $\beta\,v_o$ of the output.

Block diagram

The feedback topology admits the canonical representation shown below: a summing junction computes the error $E(s) = V_{in}(s) - \beta\,V_o(s)$, the forward path $A(s)$ amplifies the error, and the output is sampled back through the feedback element $\beta$.

Sign convention. The feedback enters the summing junction with a negative sign because it is applied to the inverting terminal of the op-amp. Physically, the differential input sees $v_+ - v_- = v_{in} - \beta v_o$.

Why feedback?

  • Gain is desensitised against variation of $A_0$: the closed-loop gain depends primarily on the resistor ratio.
  • Bandwidth is extended in direct proportion to the loop gain $A_0\beta$.
  • Input and output impedances are altered by the same factor, improving source and load matching.
  • Linearity is improved because $A(s)$ operates on the small error signal $e(t)$ rather than the full input.

05closed-loop transfer function

The feedback identity.

Three equations describe the feedback loop: the summing junction, the forward gain, and the feedback scaling. Eliminating the internal variables yields the closed-loop transfer function.

showderivation · 5 steps▶

Step 1 · loop equations

$$E(s) = V_{in}(s) - \beta\,V_o(s), \qquad V_o(s) = A(s)\,E(s).$$

Step 2 · eliminate the error

Substituting the first into the second:

$$V_o(s) = A(s)\bigl[V_{in}(s) - \beta\,V_o(s)\bigr].$$

Step 3 · gather output terms

$$V_o(s)\bigl[1 + A(s)\beta\bigr] = A(s)\,V_{in}(s).$$

Step 4 · canonical feedback formula

$$H_{cl}(s) = \frac{V_o(s)}{V_{in}(s)} = \frac{A(s)}{1 + A(s)\beta}.$$

This is Black's formula. The term $A(s)\beta$ is the loop gain; when $|A(s)\beta| \gg 1$, the closed-loop response reduces to $1/\beta$ independent of $A(s)$.

Step 5 · substitute the one-pole model

Using $A(s) = A_0/(1 + s/\omega_a)$:

$$H_{cl}(s) = \frac{A_0}{1 + s/\omega_a + A_0\beta} \;=\; \frac{A_0}{(1 + A_0\beta) + s/\omega_a}.$$

Factoring $(1 + A_0\beta)$ from the denominator:

$$H_{cl}(s) = \frac{\dfrac{A_0}{1 + A_0\beta}}{1 + \dfrac{s}{\omega_a(1 + A_0\beta)}}\;=\;\frac{G_{cl}}{1 + s/\omega_{cl}}$$

The closed-loop system remains first-order. Two quantities characterise it:

$$G_{cl} = \frac{A_0}{1 + A_0\beta}\;\xrightarrow[A_0\beta\,\gg\,1]{}\;\frac{1}{\beta}, \qquad \omega_{cl} = \omega_a\,(1 + A_0\beta)\;\xrightarrow[A_0\beta\,\gg\,1]{}\;A_0\omega_a\,\beta = \omega_t\,\beta.$$

The closed-loop DC gain $G_{cl}$ approaches the reciprocal feedback factor $1/\beta = 1 + R_2/R_1$, and the closed-loop bandwidth $\omega_{cl}$ scales linearly with $\beta$. Their product,

$$G_{cl}\cdot\omega_{cl} \;\approx\; \frac{1}{\beta}\cdot\omega_t\beta \;=\; \omega_t,$$

is conserved: feedback trades gain for bandwidth one-for-one, along a constant-GBW hyperbola.

Interpretation. The device parameter $\omega_t$ sets the maximum gain-bandwidth product. The resistor ratio sets $\beta$ and selects an operating point on the constant-GBW curve.
06closed-loop behaviour

The pole migrates outward.

The closed-loop pole is located at $s = -\omega_{cl} = -\omega_a(1 + A_0\beta)$. For realistic parameters $A_0\beta \gg 1$ and the pole is translated orders of magnitude deeper into the left half-plane. The step response is correspondingly faster; the Bode magnitude extends to higher frequencies.

$$p_{cl} = -\omega_a(1 + A_0\beta)$$

Increasing $\beta$ towards unity (the unity-gain follower, $G_{cl}\to 1$) pushes the pole out to $s = -\omega_t$. Reducing $\beta$ towards zero (high closed-loop gain) returns the pole to the open-loop position; in the limit $\beta = 0$, feedback is removed and the original pole at $-\omega_a$ is recovered.

07summary

Two transfer functions, one device parameter.

The open-loop and closed-loop transfer functions of a dominant-pole-compensated op-amp are both first-order rational functions:

$$A(s) = \frac{A_0}{1 + s/\omega_a}, \qquad H_{cl}(s) = \frac{A_0/(1 + A_0\beta)}{1 + s/\bigl[\omega_a(1 + A_0\beta)\bigr]}.$$

They differ in two respects:

  • DC gain: reduced from $A_0$ to $A_0/(1 + A_0\beta) \approx 1/\beta$.
  • Bandwidth: extended from $\omega_a$ to $\omega_a(1 + A_0\beta) \approx \omega_t\beta$.

Their product is the same, $\omega_t = A_0\omega_a$. As loop gain increases, the pole moves leftward along the negative real axis while the DC gain decreases by the same factor. The closed-loop time constant $\tau_{cl} = 1/\omega_{cl}$ is reduced by $(1 + A_0\beta)$.

The first-order model developed here is adequate for frequencies well below any higher-order internal poles. Near the unity-gain frequency of realistic devices, secondary poles introduce additional phase and the stability of the feedback loop becomes contingent on phase margin. That analysis belongs to a subsequent topic.
