Amplifier Bandwidth

Every real amplifier has a frequency limit. We'll derive where that limit comes from by modelling the amplifier's output as a simple RC low-pass filter, visualise the result on the complex plane, and build up to the Bode magnitude plot that engineers use to characterise bandwidth.

Part 01

Deriving the Transfer Function

An ideal amplifier multiplies its input by a constant gain Av at all frequencies. But real amplifiers have parasitic capacitance — stray capacitance in transistors, wiring, and load — that shunts high-frequency signals to ground. We can model this with a single resistor R and capacitor C at the output.

The output is a voltage divider between R and the capacitor impedance ZC = 1/(jωC). Working through the algebra:

  1. Start with the voltage divider: Vout/Vamp = ZC / (R + ZC)
  2. Substitute ZC = 1/(jωC): = [1/(jωC)] / [R + 1/(jωC)]
  3. Multiply top and bottom by jωC: = 1 / (1 + jωRC)
  4. Define ω0 = 1/RC, the cutoff frequency: = 1 / (1 + j(ω/ω0))
  5. Include the amplifier gain Av: H(jω) = Av / (1 + j(ω/ω0))
H(jω) = Av / (1 + j(ω/ω0))   where   ω0 = 1/RC
ω0 = 100 krad/s
f0 = 15.9 kHz
Av = 40.0 dB
The key insight: the denominator (1 + j(ω/ω0)) is a complex number that grows larger as frequency increases. Since it's in the denominator, the gain decreases. But how exactly? That's what the complex plane shows us.
Part 02

Visualising on the Complex Plane

The denominator 1 + j(ω/ω0) is a point in the complex plane. The real part is always 1. The imaginary part is ω/ω0, which grows with frequency. Sweep the test frequency below to watch the phasor grow:

Three things to notice as you increase ω:

  • The phasor gets longer. Its magnitude is √(1 + (ω/ω0)²). Since this is in the denominator of H, the gain drops.
  • The phasor tilts upward. The angle is arctan(ω/ω0). This angle becomes the phase lag of the output signal.
  • At ω = ω0, the imaginary part equals the real part (both = 1), so the magnitude is √2 ≈ 1.414. The gain is Av/√2, which is exactly −3 dB below the mid-band gain.
|H(jω)| = Av / √(1 + (ω/ω0)²)
Why −3 dB matters: −3 dB corresponds to the half-power point — the output power is exactly half of its mid-band value. Engineers define this as the bandwidth of the amplifier: the frequency range where the gain stays within 3 dB of its maximum.
Part 03

The Bode Magnitude Plot

If we compute |H| in decibels at every frequency and plot it on a log-frequency axis, we get the Bode magnitude plot — the standard way engineers visualise an amplifier's frequency response. The plot below is generated from the same transfer function.

The curve has three distinct regimes:

Passband ω ≪ ω0 — gain ≈ Av (flat)
−3 dB Cutoff ω = ω0 — gain = Av/√2
Rolloff ω ≫ ω0 — gain drops at −20 dB/decade

The −20 dB/decade slope means that for every 10× increase in frequency above ω0, the gain drops by 20 dB (a factor of 10 in voltage). This is the signature of a single-pole system — one RC time constant limiting the bandwidth.

Reading the Bode plot: The yellow marker shows your current test frequency. Everything to its left (below ω0) is the useful bandwidth where the amplifier works as intended. The green dot marks the −3 dB point — the boundary.
